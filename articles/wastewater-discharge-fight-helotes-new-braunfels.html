Wastewater Discharge Fight Moves from Helotes to New Braunfels

February 15, 2026 By Hill Country Sentinel Staff

A controversial proposal for a wastewater treatment facility, previously rejected in Helotes, has now surfaced in New Braunfels, stirring significant community concern. The project, tied to the Broken Cedar Ranch development, seeks permits to discharge up to 3.1 million gallons of treated wastewater per day into a tributary of the Guadalupe River system. If approved, this would represent a major addition to regional infrastructure, raising questions about environmental and economic impacts in Comal County.

The facility's proposed discharge volume is substantial, equivalent to the daily wastewater output of a small city. Developers argue that the treatment plant is necessary to support the planned growth of Broken Cedar Ranch, a large-scale residential and commercial project. However, local officials and environmental advocates are scrutinizing the plan, citing potential risks to water quality in the Guadalupe River, a vital natural resource for the area. The river not only supports local ecosystems but also serves as a cornerstone of the region's recreation-based economy, including tubing, fishing, and tourism.

Community opposition is gaining momentum, particularly on social media platforms where residents have organized to voice their concerns. Groups such as "Protect Our Guadalupe" have emerged, sharing information and rallying support against the discharge permit. Many locals fear that even treated wastewater could introduce pollutants or disrupt the delicate balance of the river system, potentially harming wildlife and diminishing the area's appeal as a tourist destination. "We can't risk our river for the sake of development," said Maria Lopez, a longtime New Braunfels resident and member of the opposition group.

The economic stakes are high for Comal County, where businesses reliant on river tourism contribute millions annually to the local economy. A 2022 study by the New Braunfels Chamber of Commerce estimated that river-related activities generate over $50 million in direct revenue each year. Any perceived threat to water quality could deter visitors, impacting livelihoods from outfitters to hospitality workers. Local leaders are now faced with balancing the promise of development against the preservation of a key economic driver.

The Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (TCEQ) is currently reviewing the permit application for the wastewater discharge. A public comment period is expected to open in the coming weeks, providing an opportunity for residents to weigh in on the proposal. Comal County Commissioner Jen Crownover emphasized the importance of community input, stating, "We need to ensure that any decision prioritizes the long-term health of our river and our economy. I encourage everyone to stay informed and engaged."

As the debate unfolds, the Broken Cedar Ranch wastewater proposal is shaping up to be a defining issue for New Braunfels. Environmentalists, business owners, and residents alike are bracing for a contentious process, with potential implications for how growth is managed in the rapidly expanding Hill Country region. The outcome could set a precedent for future development projects and their impact on Comal County's natural resources.
